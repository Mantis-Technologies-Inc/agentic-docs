## 1. The Problem

The Department of Defense is procuring over 1,000,000 unmanned systems (UxS) in the next 2–3 years with no purpose-built system to manage them. This is not an incremental logistics gap — it is a phase transition that existing tools, built for manned, high-value, long-lifecycle platforms, cannot address.

This problem has two branches:

**Branch A — The Warfighter:** Military units managing heterogeneous UxS fleets across multiple OEMs have no unified system of record. They cannot answer the fundamental readiness question in real time. There is no fleet visibility, no predictive maintenance, and no closed feedback loop to OEMs when systems fail in the field.

**Branch B — The UxS OEM:** Manufacturers building unmanned and autonomous systems lack a unified platform for fleet management and receive little-to-no structured performance signal back from the field once systems are deployed.

**Strategic consequence:** These gaps don't just create inefficiency — they create a national security vulnerability. Adversaries who iterate their unmanned and autonomous systems weekly will outpace militaries that iterate yearly. Mantis closes that gap.

## 4. Competitive Moat

The durable competitive advantage is not fleet visibility — that is a feature anyone can build. It is the closed loop between warfighter field data and OEM design iteration.

This network effect means:
- Every additional OEM makes the platform more valuable to warfighters
- Every additional warfighter makes the platform more valuable to OEMs

No existing tool occupies this position. Palantir and Anduril operate at the C2 and mission layer. Legacy MRO platforms are built for manned, high-value systems on long lifecycles. Nobody is building the sustainment intelligence layer purpose-built for the scale and heterogeneity of the coming UxS force.

The moat compounds only when both sides are meaningfully on the platform. OEM-first is the correct sequencing — it builds the supply side before the warfighter GTM is accessible.
